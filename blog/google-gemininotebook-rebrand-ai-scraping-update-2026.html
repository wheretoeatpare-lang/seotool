Google's NotebookLM Is Now GeminiNotebook — And Your Site Is Getting Scraped Without Attribution

📅 July 21, 2026 ⏱ 8 min read ✍️ RankSorcery Team
🤖

⚡ TL;DR

  • Google renamed NotebookLM to "Gemini Notebook" on July 16–17, 2026 — user agent changed from Google-NotebookLM to Google-GeminiNotebook.
  • If you have firewall rules or .htaccess blocks targeting the old user agent string, they stop working in August 2026. Update them now.
  • Gemini Notebook does not obey robots.txt — it's a user-triggered fetcher, not a traditional crawler.
  • The tool scrapes your content, generates audio/video from it, and sends zero referral traffic back to you.

You probably didn't notice anything happen on July 17th. No ranking drop, no manual action, no panicked thread in your SEO Slack channel. But quietly, Google changed something that affects every site owner who's been trying to control how AI tools consume their content — and if you set up a block for Google's NotebookLM fetcher, that block is now silently broken.

Here's what happened: Google renamed NotebookLM to Gemini Notebook and updated its user agent string to match. The old string, Google-NotebookLM, will keep working through August 2026 as a grace period. After that, Google says it's done. If you've hardcoded the old user agent anywhere — firewall rules, .htaccess, server configs — you've got a ticking clock on your content protection.

What Gemini Notebook Actually Does to Your Site

Let's be direct about what we're dealing with here. Gemini Notebook isn't a traditional search crawler. It's what Google calls a "user-triggered fetcher," meaning real users point it at your URLs, or it finds them automatically through its Discover Sources feature. Either way, it pulls your content, chews it up, and outputs something else entirely.

The Discover Sources Problem

The Discover Sources feature is the one that should concern you most. A user opens Gemini Notebook, types in a research topic — say, "best content strategy for SaaS startups" — and the tool automatically finds up to 10 online sources, scrapes them, and builds a research brief. Your article might be one of those sources. The user gets your insights. You get nothing.

No referral click. No citation link in a search result. No brand mention the user will remember. Your original research just became someone else's AI-generated briefing document.

The Audio and Video Problem

It gets worse. Gemini Notebook can take those scraped sources and generate a full podcast episode or a video explainer from them. Think about that for a second — your blog post, your original research, your unique takes — all of it can be reformatted into audio content that competes directly with your own stuff on platforms like YouTube or Spotify, without a single attribution back to you.

This isn't a hypothetical edge case. It's a documented, intended feature of the product. Google built it to work this way.

Why Robots.txt Won't Save You Here

A lot of site owners assume robots.txt is their safety net for AI scrapers. It's not, and this is a really important point to understand before you rely on it.

Robots.txt is a request, not a rule. Google's main Googlebot follows it because Google's business depends on maintaining good relationships with publishers. But user-triggered fetchers — including Gemini Notebook, Google-Agent (the AI agent fetcher), and similar tools — are explicitly categorized as tools that do not need to obey robots.txt.

The distinction matters: Googlebot is an autonomous crawler operating under Google's policies. Gemini Notebook's fetcher is acting on behalf of a real human user who asked it to read your page. From Google's perspective, that's functionally similar to a person typing your URL into Chrome — and you can't block that with robots.txt either.

💡 Quick Take

If you want to block Gemini Notebook (or any user-triggered fetcher), you need to do it at the server level — firewall rules or .htaccess — not with robots.txt. Robots.txt is voluntary compliance. Server-level blocks are enforced by your infrastructure.

How to Block the New GeminiNotebook User Agent

If you've already got an existing block for Google-NotebookLM, you need to add the new string alongside it — or replace it entirely before August when the old one stops being sent. Here's how to do it at the .htaccess level:

RewriteEngine On # Block Google-GeminiNotebook (new user agent, July 2026+) RewriteCond %{HTTP_USER_AGENT} Google-GeminiNotebook [NC] RewriteRule ^ - [F,L] # Block legacy Google-NotebookLM (expires August 2026 - keep for now) RewriteCond %{HTTP_USER_AGENT} Google-NotebookLM [NC] RewriteRule ^ - [F,L]

If you're running a firewall like Cloudflare, you'll need to add a rule matching the new user agent string. The logic is the same — look for Google-GeminiNotebook in the UA header and block it. The full new user agent strings (both mobile and desktop variants) are:

  • Mobile: Mozilla/5.0 (Linux; Android 10; K) AppleWebKit/537.36 (KHTML, like Gecko) Chrome/138.0.0.0 Mobile Safari/537.36 (compatible; Google-GeminiNotebook; +https://developers.google.com/crawling/docs/crawlers-fetchers/google-gemininotebook)
  • Desktop: Mozilla/5.0 (X11; Linux x86_64) AppleWebKit/537.36 (KHTML, like Gecko) Chrome/137.0.0.0 Safari/537.36 (compatible; Google-GeminiNotebook; +https://developers.google.com/crawling/docs/crawlers-fetchers/google-gemininotebook)

The easiest approach for most sites is to match just on the token Google-GeminiNotebook in the user agent string — it's present in both variants and unlikely to create false positives.

Should You Actually Block It?

This is worth thinking through rather than just reacting. Blocking isn't the automatic right move for everyone — it depends on what you're trying to protect and what you might be giving up.

Reasons to Block

  • You produce original research or proprietary data. If your competitive advantage is unique insight — studies, original data, deep analysis — letting a tool scrape it and redistribute it as audio content is a real problem.
  • You're in a space where AI-generated summaries compete directly with your content. News, newsletters, research reports — if your business model depends on people reading your content, not a Gemini Notebook summary of it, you want the block.
  • You've already invested in blocking other AI scrapers. If you blocked GPTBot, ClaudeBot, or similar crawlers for the same reasons, consistency makes sense.

Reasons Not to Block

  • Brand exposure has indirect value. If a user adds your article to their Gemini Notebook research, there's a chance they bookmark your site, look you up later, or mention you to a colleague. It's not measurable, but it's not zero.
  • You're primarily targeting AI citation visibility. If you want AI tools to cite your brand in their outputs, blocking their access sends the opposite signal. You can't get cited by a tool you've locked out.
  • Your content is already widely available and syndicated. If your content gets picked up and published elsewhere, blocking one tool won't protect it anyway.

There's no universal right answer here. But you should be making the choice deliberately, not finding out six months later that you thought you had a block in place and it stopped working when the user agent changed.

The Broader Pattern: AI Fetchers Are a Moving Target

The GeminiNotebook rename isn't a one-off. It's part of a broader pattern that's been playing out all year: AI companies ship products, those products grow into real traffic at scale, and the underlying infrastructure changes faster than most site owners can track.

Project Mariner — Google's autonomous web agent — was quietly deprecated in May 2026 and removed from Google's user-triggered fetcher documentation around the same time as this NotebookLM rename. If you were blocking that one specifically, you're now blocking a dead user agent. Your rule isn't doing anything.

OpenAI updated its own crawler documentation in July to clarify that its user agents can update their version strings over time. So a rule you wrote in January targeting a specific GPTBot version string might not match what's actually hitting your server right now.

The lesson: if you've set up AI crawler blocks, treat them like software dependencies — they need maintenance. Check your firewall logs quarterly. Look for new user agent strings you don't recognize. Cross-reference against Google's and OpenAI's official documentation for their respective fetcher lists.

What to Check Right Now

Here's a quick audit you can run today:

  1. Search your server logs for Google-NotebookLM. Are you seeing requests from it? If yes, you know it's been hitting your site. Now check when those requests stopped — that tells you how far back the rename happened in practice.
  2. Check your current firewall or .htaccess rules. Do they reference Google-NotebookLM? If that's the only string you have, your block is on a timer.
  3. Look for Google-GeminiNotebook in recent logs. If the rename happened July 17th and you haven't seen any requests since, either you've been blocking it correctly (unlikely, since the new string wouldn't match your old rule), or the tool hasn't been pointed at your site yet.
  4. Review your full AI crawler block list. While you're in there, check whether any other entries reference deprecated agents — Google-Agent pointing to Project Mariner references, old GPTBot version strings, etc.

Bottom Line

Google renamed a product and updated a user agent string. That's routine enough on its own. What makes it matter is that a lot of site owners set up specific blocks based on that exact string, and those blocks will silently fail in August when Google stops sending the old one.

Update your rules before August. Decide deliberately whether you want to block Gemini Notebook at all — don't just assume you do or don't. And if you're treating AI scraper management as a "set it and forget it" task, this is a good reminder that it isn't. The fetcher landscape changes faster than most server configs get reviewed.

Check your logs, update your rules, and know what's actually hitting your site. That's the job right now.
