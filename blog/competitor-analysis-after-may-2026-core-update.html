Why Your Competitors Are Winning After the May 2026 Core Update — And How to Close the Gap

RS
James Reyes
RankSorcery Team
· 📅 June 7, 2026 · ⏱ 12 min read · 👁 2,140 views

Five days ago, on June 2nd, Google called the May 2026 core update done. Twelve days of chaos — volatility trackers looked like seismographs during an earthquake — and now the dust is settling. Some sites came out ahead. A lot came out behind. And if you're reading this, there's a decent chance you're trying to figure out which bucket you're in, and more importantly, why some competitor of yours seems to have leapfrogged you.

I've been through enough of these updates to know the pattern. The first 48 hours after a core update completes are basically SEO grief: denial ("rankings always fluctuate"), anger ("Google is broken"), bargaining ("maybe if I add more content..."), and eventually acceptance. But the productive thing to do — the thing that actually moves the needle — is to stop staring at your own rankings and start looking at who beat you and what they did differently.

That's what this article is about. Specifically: a systematic approach to competitor analysis in the direct wake of the May 2026 core update, what signals this particular update seemed to reward, and how to turn that intelligence into action.

📅
Quick Context The May 2026 broad core update ran from May 21 to June 2, 2026 — about 12 days. It was, by most tracking tools' accounts, one of the heavier volatility events of the year, with significant movement across finance, health, e-commerce, and B2B SaaS verticals. If your rankings shifted meaningfully, you're not imagining things.

Why You Should Look at Competitors Before Your Own Site

Here's the counterintuitive thing about recovering from a core update: obsessing over your own site first is usually the wrong move. Your rankings dropped — okay. But Google didn't just punish you; it decided to promote someone else instead. The information about what Google currently wants is encoded in those promoted pages. Go there first.

Think of it like losing a job to another candidate. You could spend all week analyzing your own resume. Or you could spend an hour figuring out what the other person had that you didn't. One of those is dramatically more efficient.

The pages that gained after this update are essentially Google's revealed preference. They're case studies in what a "high quality" page looks like to Google's current algorithm. Your job is to read those case studies like they're research papers.

The 5-Step Post-Update Competitor Analysis

This is the actual process I use. It takes maybe 2–3 hours if you do it properly. Skip steps and you'll miss things.

1

Identify Your Actual Movers

Before you can analyze competitors, you need to know which of your pages lost ground and to whom. Pull a position comparison for your top 20–30 keywords between May 20 (the day before the update) and June 3 (the day after it completed). Look for anything that dropped 5+ positions. These are your investigation targets. For each losing page, note who is now ranking in position 1–3.

2

Profile the Winners

For each new top-ranking page in your target queries, open it and assess: How long is it? What's the structure? Is there original research or data? Are there author bylines with credentials? What does the page do in the first 100 words? Does it cite external sources? Note these things — don't just "feel" them. Write them down.

3

Check Their Backlink Profile Delta

Did competitors pick up meaningful backlinks in the weeks before the update? A lot of what looks like "content quality" wins are actually authority wins in disguise. Check when their strongest backlinks were acquired. If they built 15 good links in April and you built zero, that's a data point — not a content lesson.

4

Compare the Actual Content Depth

Word count matters less than comprehensiveness. Take your page and the winning competitor page and ask: what questions does their page answer that mine doesn't? What topics do they cover that I skip over? I usually do this with a simple side-by-side H2/H3 comparison. You'll often find they have an entire section you never thought to include.

5

Look at Their Technical Foundations

This update had teeth in technical areas too. Check if the winning pages have schema markup, whether their Core Web Vitals are noticeably better than yours, whether they're using canonical tags correctly. A surprising number of "content wins" are actually technical wins — their page just doesn't have the structural problems yours does.

What the May 2026 Update Actually Seemed to Reward

I want to be careful here. Google doesn't publish a changelog, and anyone who tells you they've definitively cracked what any given update rewarded is oversimplifying. That said, patterns emerge fast when you look at enough winners and losers. Here's what I'm seeing across the sites I monitor and the communities I'm in:

First-Person Experience and Original Data

Pages that shot up almost universally have something original: a proprietary study, a specific case study from their own work, or a first-person account of doing the thing the article is about. The generic "here's what the research says about keyword research" type pages got walloped. The "I ran this experiment across 47 client sites and here's what I found" pages did great. This isn't new — Google has been moving this direction since the helpful content updates — but May 2026 seems to have raised the bar another notch.

Entity Authority, Not Just Page Authority

Something I've noticed in the finance and health verticals especially: winning domains aren't just the ones with the most backlinks. They're the ones that Google seems to recognize as authoritative entities on a topic. That's a combination of your About page, your author pages, your consistent presence in the topic area over time, and your backlink anchor text. If you're a nobody in a space with thin entity signals, no amount of on-page optimization compensated for that in this update.

Content That Doesn't Stall at the "What"

Most SEO content explains what something is. The winners explain what to actually do about it, with enough specificity that you could follow their instructions without Googling anything else. That second layer — the "so what do I do now" layer — was where a lot of losers fell short. If your page stops at explaining the concept but doesn't bridge to action, that gap might be exactly what a competitor filled.

💡
The "Stranded Reader" Test Read your page as if you're a person who has never heard of the topic. At the end, ask: "Do I know what to do next?" If the answer is "sort of" or "I'd have to search for more information," your page has a depth problem that competitors probably solved.

Turning Competitor Insights Into a Content Gap Playbook

Once you've done the competitor profiling, you should have a list of things competitors are doing that you aren't. The temptation is to go fix everything at once. Don't. Triage by impact.

Here's how I prioritize the fixes list:

  • Missing entire topic sections — If a competitor's page has a whole H2 section on a subtopic you don't cover at all, that's the highest priority fix. Add it. Don't just pad the existing sections.
  • No original data or experience signals — If everything you say could have been written by someone who's never done the thing, add a case study, a specific example, or pull in proprietary data. Even a small one changes the character of the page.
  • Shallow FAQ sections vs. deep competitor FAQs — If competitors have 8–12 well-answered questions and you have 3 thin ones, expand. This is a quick win that has measurable impact on both rankings and AI Overview citations.
  • Author and entity signals missing — No author bio, no credentials visible, no About page linking to relevant work. These are surprisingly fast to fix and they compound over time.
  • Technical issues they don't have — Slow page speed, missing schema, broken canonical tags. These are often invisible to content-focused SEOs but very visible to Google. Run a proper audit.
  • Backlink gap that's too large to ignore — If a competitor has 40+ strong backlinks to a page you're trying to rank and you have 3, the content improvements alone won't get you there. You'll need a link-building plan alongside the content work.

What Most People Get Wrong in Post-Update Recovery

I want to flag a few traps I see people fall into every single time there's a major update, because they waste months of work.

Publishing Massive Quantities of New Content

The instinct is to "create more." But if the issue is that your existing pages lack depth and authority, publishing 50 more shallow pages doesn't fix that — it makes it worse. Google has a finite crawl budget for your domain and finite patience for thin content. Improve what you have before you expand what you have. This is a message that hasn't changed in five years and people still ignore it.

Completely Rewriting Pages That Don't Need It

If your page dropped a few positions but still ranks in the top 10, don't blow it up. Add missing sections, deepen existing ones, improve the experience signals. A full rewrite resets the page's history and can actually make things worse in the short term while Google re-evaluates it. Surgery, not demolition.

Blaming AI-Generated Content When That's Not the Issue

Yes, Google has been cracking down on low-quality AI content. But not all AI-assisted content underperformed. The issue isn't the production method — it's whether the output has genuine depth, original perspective, and real usefulness. If your page bombed, check the quality markers (original data, specific examples, actual expertise signals) before you assume it's about how it was written.

⚠️
Don't Panic-Publish One of the most common post-update mistakes is a flurry of new content creation in the first two weeks after an update. Wait until you've done proper competitor analysis. Publish strategically, not reactively. Core updates often have a secondary settling period 3–4 weeks after completion.

How to Track Whether Your Changes Are Working

After you've made improvements based on competitor analysis, you need a system to know whether they're working. Here's my tracking setup:

Baseline snapshot: Before you change anything, document current rankings for target keywords, the page's organic traffic in Search Console (compare 28-day to 28-day), and your key competitor's rankings for the same keywords. You need this to measure from.

Crawl verification: After changes go live, make sure Google is re-indexing the updated pages. Use Search Console's URL inspection tool and request indexing if needed. Changes you made that Google hasn't crawled are changes that don't count yet.

3-week check: After three weeks, compare current rankings to your baseline. Core update recoveries typically don't happen overnight — they happen gradually as Google re-evaluates quality signals. If you're trending up even slightly, that's a positive signal. Flat means you may need another round of improvements.

6-week full review: By week six, you should have a clear picture. If you've done meaningful competitor-informed improvements and still haven't recovered, the gap may be in domain authority or backlinks rather than content — which requires a different playbook entirely.

The Bigger Picture: Core Updates Are Now Compression Events

Here's the thing I keep coming back to after each of these updates: Google is getting better at collapsing the gap between "best in class" and "good enough." Pages that were safely ranking in positions 3–7 because nothing better existed in their niche are now getting pushed out by pages that are genuinely excellent. The tolerance for mediocre-but-technically-correct content is shrinking.

That's frustrating if you're the one getting pushed down. But it's also a massive opportunity. Most of your competitors are still publishing the same kind of content they were publishing in 2023 — structured around keyword research, written to a format, not written to an audience. If you use post-update competitor analysis not just to chase them but to genuinely leapfrog them in quality, you're building something durable.

The sites I've watched grow consistently through multiple core updates have one thing in common: they treat every update as a quality audit, not a crisis. They use competitor analysis to find out exactly where the quality bar moved, they clear it, and then they raise their own bar a little higher. That compounding advantage is hard to undo.

If you've been hit by the May 2026 update and you're feeling that sinking "what do I do now" feeling — that's actually a good sign. You're paying attention. The people who shrug these things off and don't do the work are the ones who fall further behind every cycle. Go do the competitor analysis. Figure out what the winners did. Close the gap.
