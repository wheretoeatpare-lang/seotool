YouTube Is Google's #1 Cited Source in AI Overviews — What the Data Means for Your Strategy

📅 July 23, 2026 ⏱ 9 min read ✍️ RankSorcery Team
🎥

⚡ TL;DR

  • Ahrefs tracked 3M+ US queries and found YouTube gets 21.1% of all Google AI Overview citations — more than any other source on the web.
  • Reddit is #2 at 18.5%, Facebook #3 at 10.7%. The top 4 are all platforms, not publishers — traditional editorial sites barely show up.
  • Google.com itself is #4 at 7.1%, meaning Google is increasingly citing its own properties in AI-generated answers.
  • If you don't have a YouTube presence (or Reddit activity), you're invisible to a huge chunk of AI Overview surface area — no matter how good your blog is.

Here's something that should make you rethink your entire content strategy: Ahrefs just dropped a monthly study tracking which domains get cited most in Google's AI Overviews. They analyzed over 3 million US queries spanning all topics — and the #1 most-cited source isn't The New York Times, isn't Healthline, isn't even a traditional media outlet.

It's YouTube. At 21.1% of all citations. And it's not close. Reddit comes in second at 18.5%. Facebook is third at 10.7%. Google's own properties sit at #4 with 7.1%. If you add up the top four, you're looking at well over half of all AI Overview citations going to platforms — not to the kind of editorial, link-building-optimized content most SEOs spend their days producing.

That's not a minor wrinkle. That's a structural shift in what "content" means for AI search visibility.

What the Ahrefs Data Actually Shows

Ahrefs pulls this data monthly via their Brand Radar tool, which tracks AI Overview responses across a massive set of real user queries. For the July 2026 snapshot, they looked at over 3 million US queries and counted every domain Google cited as a source.

Here's the top 10 breakdown by mention share:

  • YouTube — 21.1% (1,724,069 pages cited)
  • Reddit — 18.5% (1,060,529 pages cited)
  • Facebook — 10.7% (676,591 pages cited)
  • Google.com — 7.1% (652,536 pages cited)
  • Instagram — 5.8% (346,242 pages cited)
  • Quora — 5.0% (216,100 pages cited)
  • Wikipedia — 4.8% (117,695 pages cited)
  • Amazon — 3.5% (198,484 pages cited)
  • TikTok — 3.2% (168,608 pages cited)
  • Walmart — 1.0% (58,671 pages cited)

Everything outside the top 10 — including every publisher you've ever read about SEO strategy on — is fighting over scraps. Healthline gets 0.8%. Forbes gets 0.8%. The New York Times gets 0.4%. That's the reality.

The Platform Dominance Problem

Look at that list again. YouTube, Reddit, Facebook, Instagram, TikTok. Five of the top nine are pure social/video platforms. Wikipedia and Quora are community-generated content. Amazon and Walmart are product databases. The only "traditional editorial" site in the top 15 is Healthline — and even that is a highly structured, medically-reviewed format that's a lot closer to a database than a blog.

The typical SEO playbook — keyword research, on-page optimization, link building, publishing blog posts — produces content that's almost nowhere in this citation list. That's worth sitting with for a minute.

Why YouTube Absolutely Dominates

There are a few things going on here, and they're worth separating.

Google owns YouTube — and it shows

Part of this is structural. Google built AI Overviews. Google owns YouTube. It'd be naive to think there's zero preference baked in. When an AI Overview can cite a YouTube video with a transcript instead of a third-party article, it's going to do that — the content is machine-readable, it's on Google's own infrastructure, and it doesn't require any HTTP request to fetch.

That said, the scale matters. YouTube has 1.72 million individual pages cited. That's not Google gaming the system for a few queries — that's genuine breadth of useful video content across every topic imaginable.

Video transcripts are AI gold

Every YouTube video with auto-generated captions is basically a long-form text document Google can mine freely. A 10-minute instructional video on "how to fix a leaky faucet" has 1,200+ words of structured, step-by-step spoken content. AI Overviews are very good at extracting that and packaging it as an answer.

Meanwhile, your carefully optimized blog post on the same topic has to compete on domain authority, link profile, E-E-A-T signals, and structured data. The video just needs to exist and have transcripts.

Instructional and "how" content dominates AI Overviews

AI Overviews are most commonly triggered by how-to, what-is, and informational queries. That's exactly what YouTube is built for. Tutorials, reviews, walkthroughs, explanations — the format maps perfectly to what AI Overviews are trying to answer.

💡 Quick Take

If you're already creating written tutorials and how-to guides, you're halfway to being AI Overview-cited. The other half is making a corresponding YouTube video with good auto-captions (or uploading your own transcript). Same content, twice the citation surface area.

Why Reddit at 18.5% Should Worry You

Reddit being the #2 most-cited source isn't new news — Google has been favoring Reddit in regular search since their data licensing deal became visible in rankings around 2023-2024. But 18.5% of AI Overview citations is a reminder of how deeply embedded Reddit is in Google's source trust model.

What makes Reddit work for AI citations:

  • Real user opinions — Subreddits have authentic discussions, recommendations, and firsthand experience that AI Overviews specifically try to surface for "best X" and "is X worth it" queries.
  • High-volume, long-tail coverage — There are subreddits for literally every niche. Someone asked your question before. Reddit has an answer indexed.
  • Community consensus — An upvoted thread with 50 people agreeing on something carries social proof signals that a single-author article doesn't have.

The uncomfortable implication: if your brand, product, or topic isn't being discussed positively on Reddit, you're missing citation opportunities that no amount of on-page SEO can replace. You can't optimize your way into Reddit citations — you have to earn them by being part of communities where your expertise is genuinely useful.

The Self-Citation Problem: Google at #4

Google.com itself at 7.1% is something I haven't seen discussed enough. Over 652,000 pages on google.com got cited in AI Overviews. That's Google Maps listings, Google Shopping, Google Knowledge Graph entities, Google Business Profiles, and Google's own support docs.

This means there's a second-order visibility layer that most SEOs ignore entirely: if your business has a strong Google Business Profile, an optimized Google Maps presence, and shows up cleanly in Google's Knowledge Graph, you're getting cited in AI Overviews through google.com itself — even if your website never gets a direct citation.

It also raises a harder question: Google is building a feedback loop where it increasingly cites its own properties. That 7.1% number will likely grow over time as AI Overviews incorporate more Google Shopping, Google Maps, and Gemini-generated content.

What This Means for Your Content Strategy Right Now

Let's get practical. Here's how to actually respond to this data:

1. Start treating YouTube as a primary channel, not an afterthought

If your content strategy is blog-first, you're leaving 21% of AI Overview citation share on the table. That doesn't mean abandoning blog content — it means every significant how-to, tutorial, or explainer piece needs a corresponding video. Even a simple screen-recording walkthrough with your voice over it. Auto-captions do the rest.

Focus on instructional formats: step-by-step guides, tool walkthroughs, comparison videos, "how does X work" explanations. These are the query types that trigger AI Overviews most consistently.

2. Audit your Reddit presence (honestly)

Search your brand name and main topics on Reddit right now. What are you finding? If there's nothing — or worse, if there are negative threads you haven't engaged with — that's a citation gap. You don't need to manufacture Reddit threads (that backfires badly). But you do need to be genuinely active in relevant communities, answering questions, sharing real insights, and building a reputation as someone worth citing.

3. Don't ignore Facebook and Instagram

Facebook at 10.7% and Instagram at 5.8% are higher than any traditional publisher on the list. A lot of this is likely from public Facebook Groups, public business pages, and Instagram posts that have been indexed. Make sure your public social presence is complete, accurate, and updated. Public posts and group discussions on Facebook are very much in scope for AI Overview citations.

4. Fortify your Google-native presence

Given that Google.com itself is the #4 source, your Google Business Profile is a genuine AI citation asset. Keep it updated. Add photos, posts, Q&A answers. Make sure your GBP categories, hours, and service descriptions are accurate and complete. Google Schema markup on your site also helps get your entities properly indexed in the Knowledge Graph — which feeds directly into google.com citation data.

5. Don't abandon traditional SEO — just recalibrate

The fact that publishers like Healthline (0.8%) and Forbes (0.8%) still show up means editorial content still matters for citations. It's just that the volume concentration is in platforms. For brand visibility, technical accuracy, and niche expertise queries, a well-structured article with clear E-E-A-T signals still earns citations. The data says don't only rely on it.

The Bigger Picture: What "Content" Means in 2026

This Ahrefs study puts hard numbers on something that's been felt qualitatively for a while: the definition of "content" for AI visibility purposes isn't just blog posts anymore. It's YouTube videos, Reddit threads, Facebook groups, Instagram posts, Quora answers — essentially anywhere that humans are generating real, useful, experience-based information at scale.

The platforms that dominate AI Overview citations share a common trait: they have massive amounts of human-generated, multi-perspective, experience-based content that AI finds more trustworthy than a single-author website trying to rank for keywords. That's the core insight.

The old content strategy maxim was "create the best page on the internet for this topic." The new version might be "create the best ecosystem of content across YouTube, Reddit, and your site for this topic." That's more work — but the citation data shows it's where the AI visibility actually lives.

Bottom Line

Stop thinking about AI Overview optimization as purely a blog-and-schema game. The Ahrefs data is unambiguous: YouTube (21.1%) and Reddit (18.5%) collectively account for nearly 40% of all AI Overview citations. That's where you need to be visible. If you're not creating video content and genuinely participating in relevant communities, you're doing SEO for a surface area that doesn't represent how AI actually sources its answers.

Start with one YouTube video for your most important how-to piece of content. Make sure it has captions. Then search your key topics on Reddit and see where you can add genuine value. Those two actions alone put you ahead of most sites that are still optimizing exclusively for the traditional 10-blue-links experience that's rapidly becoming a smaller slice of Google's actual product.
