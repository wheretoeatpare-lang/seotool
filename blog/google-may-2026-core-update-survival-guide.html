The update didn't break them — it just made the cracks visible. And this one landed the morning after Google I/O 2026, where Google announced sweeping changes to AI Mode, Gemini agents, and how Search handles complex queries. The timing is not an accident.

Because honestly, the best time to act isn't after the dust settles. It's now.

What Is the May 2026 Core Update (and Why It's Different)

Google's official description is deliberately vague: "a regular update designed to better surface relevant, satisfying content for searchers from all types of sites." That's almost word-for-word what they said about the March update. They haven't published a companion blog post, haven't pointed to any new ranking signals, and haven't issued guidance specific to this update. If you're waiting for a Google spokesperson to tell you exactly what changed, you're going to be waiting a long time.

What we know from watching core updates across the past three years is this: Google doesn't add new signals with core updates. What it does is recalibrate the weights — how much each existing signal matters relative to the others. Think of it like adjusting the equalizer on a music system. The instruments (ranking factors) are the same. But suddenly the bass is louder, or the treble is cranked up, and the song sounds completely different.

The March 2026 update was notable for one specific shift: it moved ranking from an absolute scoring model toward a comparative one. Pages are no longer evaluated just on their own merits — they're constantly stacked against every other page competing for the same query. This is why you can have a piece of content that hasn't changed in six months suddenly drop ten positions. Your page didn't get worse. A competitor's page just got relatively better, and the algorithm noticed.

The May update appears to be continuing and deepening that same direction. Sites that already strengthened their E-E-A-T signals after March are largely holding ground. Sites that were in "wait and see" mode are now seeing the second hammer drop. And the timing against Google I/O 2026 is worth thinking about — when Google announces that AI Mode and Gemini agents are going to fundamentally change how Search answers complex queries, they simultaneously need the underlying index to be filled with high-quality, trustworthy content. A core update right after I/O isn't a coincidence. It's preparation.

Who's Winning, Who's Losing, and Why

Early data from SERP tracking tools and community reports is painting a pretty clear picture. The winners aren't surprising if you've been paying attention. Reddit, Yelp, Quora, and major retailer sites like HomeDepot are picking up significant visibility. These are all platforms with massive user-generated content ecosystems, strong engagement signals, and — critically — real human experiences embedded in the content. When someone posts on Reddit about a product they bought, that's exactly the kind of first-person experience Google has been saying it wants to surface since the original helpful content updates.

The losers are more varied, but a few patterns are obvious. Thin affiliate content sites are getting hammered. Sites that built out large content libraries by covering every possible variation of a keyword — but without actual depth on any of them — are seeing across-the-board drops. DIY forums with older, less-maintained content are also falling. And here's one that will hurt some of you: sites that published a lot of AI-written content in 2025 as a traffic play, without proper editing or genuine expert input, are now paying for it.

The E-E-A-T Squeeze Is Real

I've seen this pattern a hundred times with previous updates, and this one is no different: the sites that invested in showing genuine Experience and Expertise are doing fine. Not just claiming expertise in an author bio that says "John is a digital marketing expert with 10 years of experience" — but actually demonstrating it through specific, first-hand details that a non-expert couldn't write.

Think about the difference between an article that says "Core updates can affect your rankings, so it's important to have quality content" versus one that walks you through exactly which Google Search Console metrics to pull, what a normal range looks like, and what a core update pattern looks like compared to a manual penalty. The first one is technically correct and totally useless. The second one could only be written by someone who has actually done this work. Google has gotten much better at telling the difference.

The Discover Effect Nobody's Talking About

One thing worth highlighting: Google's own documentation states that core updates can influence Google Discover, featured snippets, and other Search features in addition to standard organic rankings. If you rely on Discover traffic — which we covered in depth in our Google Discover 2026 guide — this update may have hit you in places Search Console doesn't make immediately obvious. Discover impressions can drop overnight without any change to your traditional rankings. Check both your "Search type: Web" and "Search type: Discover" segments in Search Console separately.

The Real Reason Your Rankings Dropped (Hint: It's Not What You Think)

Let me be honest with you about something that most SEO writeups skip over: a lot of people are going to spend the next two weeks convinced they need to rewrite every piece of content on their site. That's not what's happening here, and doing that would be a mistake.

The comparative ranking model means your drop often has almost nothing to do with your content getting worse. It's about competitors in your niche publishing better content, earning more links, accumulating more user engagement signals — and then the algorithm adjusting to reflect that new competitive landscape. You could have the same page you had six months ago, and if three new players have entered your niche and published genuinely great content, you're going to move down even if you did nothing wrong.

This is actually important, because it changes what you should do about it. Blindly rewriting your top pages based on a traffic drop is like repainting your house because the neighbor built a nicer one. What you need to do is understand specifically where you've fallen behind relative to what's now ranking above you — and make targeted improvements based on that analysis.

"Your page didn't necessarily get worse. What changed is that Google got better at measuring how much better your competitors are."

I've audited dozens of sites after core updates, and the single most useful thing you can do in the first week is run a competitive gap analysis on your affected pages. Look at what's ranking above you now that wasn't there three weeks ago. Is their content longer? More specific? Do they have author credentials you're missing? Do they cite primary sources where you cite other blogs? That gap analysis tells you exactly what work to do.

This is also why the Competitor Analyzer on RankSorcery is worth using right now — specifically to surface which competing pages are outranking yours and what they're doing differently. Guessing at this stuff wastes weeks. Looking at the data takes twenty minutes.

Your Step-by-Step Recovery and Improvement Plan

Okay, the update will wrap up around June 4. Once it does, here's the exact process I'd follow. Not the generic "write better content" advice, but the actual sequence that makes a difference.

The goal isn't to reverse-engineer the algorithm. The goal is to make your site genuinely better than what's currently outranking you, in ways that matter to your actual readers. That's it. Everything else is noise.

1

Run a Full SEO Audit First

Before you touch a single page, run a full technical audit to rule out technical issues that look like algorithm penalties but aren't. Crawl errors, broken internal links, slow page speeds, and duplicate content can all cause ranking drops that get blamed on core updates. Use RankSorcery's SEO Auditor to get a full site diagnostic in under a minute — it'll tell you if there are technical problems to fix before you start changing content.

2

Identify Your Actual Losers in Search Console

In Google Search Console, go to Search Results → compare date range (last 28 days vs. previous 28 days). Sort by "Clicks Difference" to find your biggest losers. Export the top 20 URLs that dropped most. These are your priority pages. Don't try to fix everything at once — focus on what was driving traffic.

3

Analyze What's Now Outranking You

For each priority page, Google the target keyword and actually read the top 3 results carefully. Ask yourself: What specific information does that page have that yours doesn't? Is there first-hand experience, original data, specific examples, or expert commentary that you're missing? This is where the competitive gap is — and it's almost always something you can actually add.

4

Strengthen E-E-A-T Signals on High-Priority Pages

Add real credentials to author bylines. Link to primary sources rather than citing other blog posts. Add a "Last updated" date and actually update the content. Include specific examples from your own experience. Add a FAQ section addressing real questions from your audience. If the topic involves health, finance, or legal content — the YMYL categories — treat E-E-A-T as a hard requirement, not a nice-to-have.

5

Check Your Keyword Opportunities Post-Update

Core updates reshuffle SERPs, and some of your competitors may now be weaker on certain terms they used to own. Use the Keyword Volume Checker to find related search terms where there's now a gap to fill. Some of the best post-update opportunities are in secondary keywords around your main topics, where the new SERP landscape has opened space for fresh content.

What to Stop Doing Right Now

There's an equally important part of this conversation that doesn't get enough attention: the things you should actively stop doing after a core update. I've watched site owners make their situations significantly worse by reacting badly to algorithm changes, and I don't want that to happen to you.

First: stop mass-deleting content. Yes, content pruning can help — we wrote about this in our content pruning guide — but there's a big difference between strategically removing low-quality pages after careful analysis and panicking and deleting 40% of your site because you lost traffic. Deleting the wrong pages can trigger a crawl budget problem, lose you internal link equity, and — if you don't redirect properly — create a wave of 404 errors that tanks your technical health score.

Second: stop chasing AI content shortcuts as a response to an AI update. I know it's tempting when you see the volume of work involved in improving your existing pages. But this is exactly the wrong direction. Google has become significantly better at identifying AI-generated content that lacks genuine experience and depth. Publishing more of it as a "recovery strategy" is like trying to put out a fire with gasoline.

Third: stop comparing your traffic to one week ago. Rankings during a live core update are genuinely noisy. A page can drop ten positions, then recover seven, then drop four more — all within a single week. If you make content decisions based on that volatility, you will spend two weeks chasing a phantom. Wait for the rollout to complete. Wait another week after that. Then look at the data.

  • Wait for Google to confirm the rollout is complete before taking any major action
  • Run a technical SEO audit to rule out technical issues before assuming content problems
  • Compare your content against what's now ranking #1 for your target keywords — specifically
  • Add real first-hand experience, primary sources, and author credentials to affected pages
  • Use competitor analysis to find gaps rather than guessing at what's changed
  • Check both Web and Discover traffic separately in Search Console — they're different
  • Look for keyword opportunities that have opened up due to competitor rankings shifting
  • Don't mass-delete content, mass-rewrite content, or publish a flood of new AI articles as a "fix"

The sites that come out of core updates stronger are almost never the ones who reacted fastest. They're the ones who waited, analyzed, and made targeted improvements based on what the data actually showed. That's it. That's the whole strategy.

Nobody talks about this, but the best thing you can do after a Google core update is also the hardest: slow down. Don't panic-publish. Don't restructure your entire site. Audit carefully, act surgically, and give the changes time to register. Google crawls content on its own schedule, and a rewritten page that's genuinely better than the competition will recover — but probably not this week. Think in months, not days.
