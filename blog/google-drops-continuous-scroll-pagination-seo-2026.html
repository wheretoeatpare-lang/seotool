Google Killed Continuous Scroll — And Page 2 Just Got a Second Chance

RS
James Reyes
RankSorcery Team
· 📅 June 28, 2026 · ⏱ 9 min read · 👁 2,134 views

I have a ranking I genuinely hate checking. Position 11. The first result on page 2. For years, in my head, position 11 was the same as not ranking at all — a graveyard where perfectly good content went to collect dust and generate exactly zero organic traffic.

That assumption just got a lot more complicated. In late June 2026, Google officially announced it's scrapping continuous scroll on both desktop and mobile search results. Instead of the endless feed users have had since 2022, we're going back to the paginated format — ten blue links (and the usual SERP chaos) per page, with a "Next" button at the bottom.

The SEO community's reaction was predictably all over the place. Half the crowd treated it like the return of a lost king. The other half immediately started arguing about whether page 2 even matters anymore with AI Overviews eating the top of page 1. Both camps are missing the actual story.

⚡
The Quick Version Google ended continuous scroll in late June 2026, returning to traditional paginated results for both desktop and mobile. This changes CTR curves, click psychology, and — most importantly — what it means to rank at positions 11–20.

What Actually Happened (And When)

Google introduced continuous scroll on mobile in 2021, brought it to desktop in early 2023, and for three-plus years it was the default. Instead of hitting "Next" to see more results, users just scrolled and results loaded automatically below.

The stated reason for the reversal? According to the announcement, Google's testing showed that continuous scroll was actually worse for finding relevant results — people scrolled past content without properly evaluating it, and the format increased the mental load of search. The old paginated model, it turns out, creates natural stopping points that help searchers process what they're looking at.

There's almost certainly an ads angle too. Paginated results make it much easier for Google to surface ads in predictable, high-visibility positions at the top of each page — a structure that's far more advertiser-friendly than an endless scroll format. But that's speculation, and I can't prove it, so I'll park it here.

The CTR Cliff Is Back — And It's Steeper Now

When continuous scroll was active, the drop-off between position 10 and position 11 was softened. Results below the fold loaded automatically. A distracted user might scroll past them, but they at least had a chance of being seen.

With pagination back, position 11 is now behind a deliberate friction point: a user must actively click "Next" to see it. Research from the pre-continuous-scroll era consistently showed that fewer than 1% of searchers go to page 2. Some studies put it at 0.6%. That cliff is probably back, and it may actually be steeper this time because users have been trained by continuous scroll to expect results to load automatically — clicking "Next" now requires a mental shift.

"The difference between position 10 and position 11 used to be a gentle slope. With pagination, it's a ledge."

This has two clear implications. First, positions 1–10 just got significantly more valuable. If you're sitting at 11–15 for any term that drives meaningful traffic, you need to take that seriously right now — those positions will almost certainly see CTR drops in the coming weeks as user behavior re-adjusts to the new format.

Second, any SEO strategy that was relying on "pretty good" positioning — ranking in the 8–12 range and doing okay because scroll meant some visibility — is going to feel this in Search Console data over the next month.

Your SERP Snippet Now Has to Work Harder

Here's the thing people aren't talking about enough: page 1 just became a much more competitive real estate with a cleaner visual separation from everything below it. That means the ten results on page 1 are going to be compared more directly against each other by users who are not just passively scrolling, but actively reading and deciding.

Your title and meta description — your "snippet" — now carry even more weight. A sloppy, auto-generated meta description that Google rewrites poorly might have survived when users were mindlessly scrolling. In a paginated world where your snippet is competing cleanly against nine others on a defined page, it needs to be sharp.

💡
The snippet problem most sites have Most sites have no idea what their actual SERP snippet looks like to a real user on desktop vs. mobile. They write a meta description, assume it shows up, and never check. Google rewrites descriptions on roughly 70% of queries. You need to see what's actually appearing — and make sure it earns the click.

I've been running RankSorcery's SERP Snippet Previewer on client pages this week specifically to audit what their snippets look like in the new paginated context. The difference between a well-crafted snippet and a generic one is striking when you see them side by side in a mock SERP view. If your title is truncated at an awkward point or your description reads like it was written for a database field, you're losing clicks you should be winning.

Is Page 2 Actually Dead or Just Mostly Dead?

Let me give you the honest answer: page 2 is mostly dead for short, competitive commercial queries. If someone's searching "best CRM software" or "cheap flights to Bali," they are never going to click Next. Full stop.

But there's a specific category where page 2 has always been underappreciated — long-tail, research-oriented queries where users are trying to build a comprehensive understanding of a topic. Someone searching "how does hreflang affect subdomain crawling" is more likely to page through results than someone searching "buy running shoes." The former is digging; the latter is shopping.

So the stakes of being on page 2 vs. page 1 depend heavily on the query type. Run your keyword mix through that filter before panicking. You might find that most of your page-2 rankings are on exactly the kind of queries where page 2 still gets legitimate traffic.

What to Actually Do Right Now

Here's my prioritized action list. This isn't theoretical — these are the things I'm doing for sites right now.

1

Pull Your Position 8–15 Rankings Immediately

Go into Google Search Console, filter by position 8–15, and sort by impressions. These are your highest-risk pages — they're either just hanging on to page 1 or sitting at the very top of the newly punishing page 2. Any page with significant impressions at those positions needs attention now, not after CTR drops start showing in your data.

2

Audit Every Snippet for Truncation and Quality

Use a snippet previewer to check that your title tags aren't being cut off in ugly places and that your meta descriptions are readable, specific, and benefit-led. "We offer comprehensive solutions for your business needs" is the kind of description that'll get you zero clicks in a competitive page-1 lineup. Be specific. Name the benefit. Use the character budget you're given.

3

Refresh Any Page-2 Content That Deserves Better

If you have solid content ranking at position 11–15 on a query you care about, now is the best possible time to push it over the line. Add a section that directly answers a related subtopic. Update statistics. Add internal links from stronger pages. Small improvements at position 11 are worth more effort now than they were a month ago.

4

Don't Abandon AI Search Optimization Either

One thing the pagination-vs-scroll debate is obscuring: AI Overviews still appear above the blue links on a huge percentage of queries. Pagination matters for traditional organic results, but you still need your content to be citation-worthy for AI answers if you want total SERP visibility. These are not competing strategies — they're both necessary.

5

Watch Your CTR Curve in Search Console for the Next 30 Days

User behavior is going to take weeks to re-adjust to paginated results. Set up a comparison in Search Console — today vs. 30 days from now — and watch specifically for CTR changes at positions 8–12. Those position ranges will be the most volatile as the transition settles.

SERP Snippet Optimization Checklist

Since snippet quality is going to matter more than ever in a paginated SERP, here's what I check on every important page:

  • Title tag is under 60 characters and doesn't truncate at a confusing word
  • Title includes the primary keyword near the front
  • Meta description is 130–155 characters and reads like a human wrote it
  • Meta description includes a clear benefit or reason to click — not just a description of what the page is
  • Title doesn't duplicate exactly what the H1 says (slight variation helps)
  • No keyword stuffing in either field — Google will rewrite it and it usually goes worse
  • Favicon is displaying correctly in Chrome (showing in SERPs since 2019, still matters for brand recognition)
  • URL is readable and not a string of parameters or gibberish
  • Preview looks clean on both desktop and mobile character widths
🧠
One more thing worth knowing Google has quietly reduced how often it rewrites title tags compared to 2022–2023 — but it still rewrites meta descriptions constantly. Your best defense is writing descriptions that are already better than what Google would generate: specific, action-oriented, and under 155 characters. When your description is already genuinely good, Google tends to leave it alone.

The Bigger Picture: Search UI Is Changing Fast

Stepping back from the immediate tactical stuff — this change is a reminder that the SERP itself is not fixed. Google redesigns how results look and behave constantly, and every redesign reshuffles who wins and who loses click-through.

We've had a few years where the conventional wisdom was "ranking doesn't matter as much anymore because the SERP is too cluttered with AI answers, ads, and featured snippets." There's truth to that. But pagination is a reminder that the fundamental logic of page 1 vs. page 2 still applies to the organic links that do appear — and that those links need to earn their clicks more aggressively than ever.

My honest take: this is ultimately a positive change for users who want to deliberately browse search results, and a negative one for anyone coasting on mediocre page-1 positions. If your snippet is weak and your content is barely hanging on at position 9 or 10, this is a good forcing function to finally fix it.

Position 11 is still mostly dead. But positions 1–10 just got a little more meaningful. And in a SERP where AI is eating traffic from the top and pagination is enforcing a hard cut at the bottom, getting your snippet right is no longer optional.
