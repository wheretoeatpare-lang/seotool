Google May 2026 Core Update: Winners, Losers & Recovery Guide

RS
James Reyes
RankSorcery Team
· 📅 May 24, 2026 · ⏱ 10 min read · 👁 1,847 views

Google dropped the May 2026 core update on May 21st, and it's still rolling out as I'm writing this. I've been watching the data across about 40 sites I either manage directly or have access to through clients — and the volatility is real. Some sites got a 30-40% bump overnight. Others dropped off a cliff. And a handful of them? Completely untouched, which honestly tells its own story.

This is the second broad core update of 2026, coming just two months after the March update wrapped. That's a tight turnaround, and from what I'm seeing in the data, it feels like Google is doubling down on signals that the March update started rewarding. Not a reversal — more like a reinforcement. Let me walk you through what's actually changing and what you should be doing right now while the rollout is still underway.

What's Actually Rolling Out Right Now

Google's official line is pretty standard: this is a "broad core update designed to better surface relevant, satisfying content for searchers from all types of sites." Not exactly illuminating. What they're not saying — but what the data is clearly showing — is that this update heavily weights content depth, author credibility signals, and something I'd describe as "earned authority" rather than manufactured authority.

The rollout started globally on May 21st and Google says it can take up to two weeks to complete. So we're still roughly halfway through. That matters because you might not have seen your full impact yet. Sites I track showed initial movement within 48 hours, then another wave around 96 hours in. Expect more flux over the next week.

Who Got Hit — and Who Didn't

Based on data from SEMrush Sensor, Sistrix, and my own tracking across multiple niches, here's what I'm seeing in the early days:

The Losers (So Far)

AI content farms are getting absolutely hammered. I'm talking sites that publish 15-20 AI-generated posts per day with no real editorial oversight. Several of these lost 60-80% of their organic visibility in the first 72 hours. Not surprising — Google has been tightening the screws on this since the March update and this one seems to have a sharper blade.

Affiliate review sites with thin comparison content are down across the board. If your entire value proposition is "here are 10 products with affiliate links and a 200-word summary for each," you probably had a bad week. The pattern is consistent across tech, home goods, and health niches.

News aggregator sites — the ones that republish press releases verbatim or lightly rewrite wire stories — also took serious hits. This surprises some people, but it makes sense. If you're not adding original reporting or insight, you're just noise.

The Winners

Independent publishers with genuine subject matter expertise are winning big. I'm seeing 20-45% traffic increases on sites where a real person with demonstrable experience is the primary author. Think niche blogs, technical documentation sites, and specialized industry publications.

E-commerce sites with robust product pages — actual reviews, real usage context, comparison data from genuine testing — are doing well too. It's not just about the words on the page; it's about the signals surrounding them.

Interestingly, I'm also seeing smaller YouTube channels with companion blog content winning. The cross-platform authority signal seems to be working. If your blog reinforces a YouTube presence where you demonstrably show up as an expert, Google seems to give that extra weight right now.

🔍
Controversial TakeA lot of SEOs are blaming "AI content" for their drops, but plenty of the losers I've analyzed published mostly human-written content. The real problem is thin content — regardless of how it was created. AI-generated content done well, with real expertise layered in, is surviving just fine. Stop blaming the tool and look at the output quality.

The Pattern Separating Winners From Losers

I've done a lot of manual reviews this week, and after going through roughly 60 affected URLs across different niches, one pattern keeps emerging: depth and specificity at the page level. Not just "more words" — deeper, more specific, more contextually anchored content that couldn't have been written by someone without actual experience in the subject.

"Google isn't rewarding 'good writing.' It's rewarding specific, experiential knowledge that could only come from someone who's actually done the thing."

Here's a concrete example. I compared two recipe sites — both publish similar content, both have been around for years. Site A publishes recipes with detailed troubleshooting notes ("if your sauce breaks at this step, here's exactly why and how to fix it"), real texture and flavor descriptions, and personal anecdotes from actual cooking experience. Site B publishes technically correct recipes with no personality or depth. Site A is up 31%. Site B is down 28%. Same niche, same content type, wildly different outcomes.

E-E-A-T Is Being Evaluated Differently Now

Experience is the "E" that's doing the heavy lifting in this update. Not just displayed credentials or author bios — Google's Quality Raters are specifically looking for evidence of lived experience in the content itself. Things like: first-person problem-solving, specific edge cases and how to handle them, "I tested this and here's what actually happened" language.

This is actually good news for smaller sites with genuine expertise. You don't need to be a massive publication to win here. You need to write like someone who has genuinely wrestled with the topic rather than researched it from the outside.

The AI Content Problem Isn't Going Away

I use AI tools in my content workflow — I'm not going to pretend otherwise. But the key phrase is "in my workflow." AI as a first draft or outline, human expertise and experience layered on top, real editing pass, original insights added throughout. That combination is surviving this update fine.

What's dying is the "AI generates entire article, editor hits publish" pipeline. Those pages lack the specificity, the micro-examples, the genuine opinion that comes through when a human who knows the subject actually writes. Google seems to be getting better at detecting this absence. And honestly, so can readers — which probably feeds into the engagement signals Google's using.

How to Tell If Your Site Was Affected

First, don't just look at overall traffic. Core updates typically hit specific page types or content categories, not your entire site uniformly. I've seen sites where one content type tanked 40% while another category held steady or improved. You need to dig into the page-level data.

Here's exactly how I'd check your situation right now:

1

Compare May 21–24 vs. the previous 3-day period

In Google Search Console, filter by date range. Look at impressions and clicks side by side. A sudden cliff drop starting May 21 is a strong indicator you were hit by this update specifically.

2

Segment by page type or content category

Break down traffic by URL patterns (e.g., /blog/, /reviews/, /guides/). This tells you which content type got hit versus which is stable. Don't try to fix everything — prioritize the affected segments.

3

Pull your top 20 affected landing pages

Sort by biggest traffic decline over the same period. These are your highest-priority pages to review. Open each one and ask yourself honestly: "Would a reader consider this better than what's currently ranking above me?"

4

Check your competitors' movement

Who replaced you in the SERPs? Are they larger brands, niche specialists, or fresh content? The answer tells you whether you're facing a site authority problem, a content quality problem, or something else entirely.

5

Wait for the rollout to finish before making major decisions

Google says up to 2 weeks. Rankings are still fluctuating. Auditing now is smart — but hold off on mass redirects, content deletions, or structural changes until the dust settles around June 4th.

Your Recovery Playbook

If you've been hit, here's the honest truth: you're probably not recovering during this rollout. Core update recoveries typically happen at the next core update — which, given this year's pace, could be as soon as July or August 2026. That gives you roughly 8-10 weeks to make meaningful improvements.

Audit Your Existing Content Ruthlessly

Stop publishing new content for two weeks. I mean it. Use that bandwidth to go through your existing pages and categorize them: worth keeping as-is, worth improving, worth consolidating, worth removing entirely. Most sites I audit have 30-40% of their content that's actively hurting their authority signals by dragging down the overall quality perception of the site.

Add Real Depth to Your Top-Priority Pages

Pick your 15-20 highest-traffic pages that lost the most ground. For each one, add:

  • At least one section based on direct experience — "when I tested this" or "here's what I found when I actually did this"
  • Specific numbers, dates, context that couldn't have been fabricated or generically produced
  • Genuine opinion — take a position, even if it's controversial within your niche
  • An updated "last verified" date — and make sure the content actually reflects current reality
  • A named author with a real bio that includes relevant credentials or first-hand experience
  • Internal links to more specific, related content on your site to build topical depth

Fix Your Thin Pages Before Google Does It For You

This is where most people get squeamish. If you have pages with under 400 words that don't genuinely serve a user need — combine them, expand them, or delete them. Every thin page on your site is a signal to Google about your overall quality standards. One weak page doesn't tank your site, but 200 of them will.

⚠️
Don't Do This During RecoveryDon't 301 redirect your dropped pages to your homepage or a category page. This is a panicked move that signals even more quality problems. If a page can't be improved, either expand it meaningfully or delete it and let the 404 resolve naturally over time. Redirecting to irrelevant pages is worse than a 404.

What to Do Right Now, Before the Rollout Finishes

The rollout is still in progress. That means Google is still re-crawling and re-evaluating pages. This is actually a narrow window where some quick wins are possible — not guaranteed, but possible if you can get improvements crawled before the update fully settles.

Focus right now on your pages that are borderline — ones that dropped a bit but didn't completely fall out of the top 20. These are often fixable within the current rollout window if you can improve them fast enough to catch a re-crawl. Pages that dropped from page 1 to page 5+ almost certainly won't recover until the next update regardless of what you do today.

  • Update the publication date on recently-improved pages to signal freshness for re-crawling
  • Add FAQ sections to long-form content — pull ideas directly from Google's "People Also Ask"
  • Strengthen author bio pages with actual experience proof points: publications, case studies, certifications
  • Check that your schema markup is valid and relevant — Article, Person, and FAQPage schemas carry weight right now
  • Fix any Core Web Vitals issues, especially INP on JavaScript-heavy pages — CWV is a real signal, just less talked about
  • Pull your Search Console coverage report and resolve any indexing issues blocking your best content pages

Here's the thing people always miss about core updates: the recovery isn't just about content quality. Google is re-evaluating your entire site's relationship with searchers. That includes your engagement metrics, your brand search volume, your backlink velocity — all of it feeds the model. So alongside content improvements, build real brand presence. Show up on social, earn genuine mentions, get traffic from sources other than Google.

It's a slow process. Anyone promising you a 2-week recovery from a core update hit is selling you something. But if you do the work consistently over the next 8 weeks, you'll be in a strong position when the next update lands — and based on this year's pace, that's not far off.

Stay calm, stay methodical, and do the actual work. The sites that recover are almost always the ones that looked at the data honestly and made real improvements — not the ones that panicked, mass-deleted pages, or threw a thousand new posts at the wall hoping something sticks.
