On June 5, 2026, Google quietly dropped one of the most significant changes to how brands appear in search — and I'm genuinely surprised it hasn't gotten more attention. Google Search Profiles went officially live for publishers and creators, giving them a dedicated, shareable profile page right inside Google Search at profile.google.com/@yourname. It shows your latest articles, videos, social posts, and a follow button that feeds directly into Discover.

I've seen a lot of new Google features come and go, and most of them don't matter as much as the announcements suggest. This one is different. Here's the thing — it's not just a vanity profile page. It signals a fundamental shift in how Google is thinking about identity and authority in search. And when you understand the bigger picture, you realize that Google Search Profiles are just one visible piece of a much larger rethinking of what "brand authority" means in the age of AI Overviews and AI Mode.

In this article I'm going to break down exactly what Google Search Profiles are, why brand authority is more critical to SEO in 2026 than it's been in years, who can claim a profile today, and — most importantly — what you can do right now whether or not you qualify. Because the deeper game here isn't about a profile page. It's about understanding what Google actually rewards when only 27.6% of searches result in a click to the open web and AI Overviews appear on over 13 billion queries per month.

What Google Search Profiles Actually Are (And Why You Should Care)

Let me be honest with you — the name "Search Profiles" is almost aggressively boring for what is actually a meaningful change to how publishers and creators exist within Google's ecosystem. For the past year or two, Google had been quietly testing what they were calling "Discover Publisher Pages." Now they've rebranded, formalized, and opened up the feature under the Search Profiles name.

Here's how it works: if you qualify, you can go to creators.google/profile, claim your profile, and customize it with your avatar, bio, website links, social media accounts, and video platforms. Once set up, your profile becomes accessible in a few ways: through your Knowledge Panel in Google Search (that box on the right side when someone searches your name or brand), by tapping your name on Google Discover, and through a direct URL people can share or bookmark.

The profile page itself aggregates your latest content — articles from your site, YouTube videos, posts from connected social platforms — all in one place. There's also a Follow button that, when tapped, means that Discover will actively serve your content to that person going forward. That last part is worth pausing on. Discover is a significant traffic source for publishers that often goes underreported. Getting someone to follow your profile on Google is functionally like getting a newsletter subscriber — except it's built into the world's most used search app and requires zero friction from the user.

What really caught my eye, though, was this line from Google's official announcement: "Claiming a profile may trigger the creation of a Knowledge Panel for eligible publishers and creators. If you already have a Knowledge Panel, it will be enhanced with your updated avatar, latest content, and a direct profile link." So claiming a Search Profile can actually create a Knowledge Panel for your brand where one didn't exist before. That's a significant E-E-A-T signal — Knowledge Panels are one of the clearest ways Google signals to users (and to its own systems) that a brand is a recognized, real entity.

Even if your profile doesn't become visible to most users immediately, the internal entity recognition that comes with a verified profile has downstream effects on how Google treats your content across all of search — including AI Overviews.

The requirements to claim a profile are specific: you need a minimum number of subscribers or followers on at least one major content platform — 100,000 on YouTube, Instagram, or X (formerly Twitter), or 300,000 on TikTok. That immediately disqualifies most small businesses and niche publishers. But stay with me here, because the implications of Search Profiles reach far beyond the people who can claim one today.

The Real Reason Brand Authority Is Now the #1 SEO Signal

Here's the thing that I don't see talked about enough: Google didn't invent Search Profiles in a vacuum. They're a response to a structural shift in how search works — and that shift should affect how every SEO thinks about their strategy, profile or no profile.

Think about the math for a second. AI Overviews appear on an estimated 13 billion queries per month globally. Google AI Mode crossed 1 billion monthly active users as of mid-2026. According to the latest data, only 27.6% of Google searches send someone to an actual website on the open web. In this environment, what does it mean to "rank"? Traditional position #1 doesn't guarantee you get the click anymore. What Google is increasingly doing — through AI Overviews, AI Mode, and Discover — is serving content it trusts, from sources it recognizes, to users who have demonstrated interest in those sources.

That shift fundamentally changes the SEO equation. For years, you could build topical authority primarily through content — write enough good articles on your niche, earn links, and Google would eventually recognize you as an authority. That approach still matters. But now there's a second layer: does Google recognize your brand as an entity? Is your brand something users actively search for, follow, and return to? Are you being cited in AI Overviews and AI Mode responses? Those are brand authority signals, and they're increasingly driving which sites get featured versus which ones get replaced by an AI-generated summary.

Research from Nico Digital's prompt audits across their 175+ client portfolio found something that should stick with every SEO: branded search lift is the single most reliable leading indicator of AI search citation frequency. When AI Overviews and LLMs start citing a brand regularly, that brand's branded search volume in Google Search Console rises 60 to 90 days later. It works the other way too — brands that actively build brand recognition start showing up more in AI answers. The two dynamics are feeding each other.

Why BrightEdge Data Should Change How You Think About Ranking

BrightEdge published data earlier this year showing that sources cited in Google AI Overviews strongly correlate with top-10 organic ranking on the same query. That sounds obvious — if you rank top 10, you get cited in the AI summary. But the inverse isn't true. Ranking top 10 doesn't guarantee AI citation. BrightEdge found that brand authority and content structure are the two factors that most differentiate who gets cited versus who gets passed over, even among top-ranking pages.

In other words: two pages can both rank on page 1 for the same query. The one with stronger brand signals — more recognition, more entity clarity, more consistent author and publisher identity — gets cited in AI Overviews. The other one gets a blue link that fewer than 28% of users click on. That's the world we're operating in right now, and it's exactly why brand authority matters even to sites that will never qualify for a Search Profile.

Who Can Claim a Profile — And What to Do If You Can't Yet

The 100,000-follower threshold for Search Profiles is going to frustrate a lot of people, and honestly — the frustration is valid. The requirement means this feature is initially restricted to established creators, large media brands, and companies that have already invested in social audience-building. If you're a B2B software company or a niche publisher with 8,000 Twitter followers and a loyal email list, you don't qualify today. That doesn't mean you're locked out of the underlying benefits, but it does mean you need a different path to get there.

If you don't qualify for a Search Profile today, the most impactful thing you can do is focus on what I call entity clarity. Google needs to understand who you are as a brand — not just what keywords you target. That means having a consistent brand name across all web properties, building out your About page with clear authorship and Organization schema markup, getting editorial mentions in established publications, and creating a Wikidata entity if you don't have one.

Wikidata is probably the most underutilized brand authority tool in SEO right now. A clean Wikidata entity — even a basic one with your brand name, founding date, description, and website — feeds into how knowledge graph systems across Google, ChatGPT, and other AI engines recognize and classify your brand. It's free, it takes a few hours to set up, and I've consistently seen it move the needle on Knowledge Panel creation within weeks for brands that had none before.

The Workaround That Actually Works

For brands that don't qualify for a Search Profile, treat your site's About page and your key author pages as the equivalent of a publisher profile. Structure them with Person or Organization schema markup, link them to your social profiles using sameAs properties, and keep them genuinely updated with recent content. Google's entity recognition systems actively read this data when deciding whether to surface a brand in Knowledge Panels and AI Overviews.

I'll add one more thing: the 100K threshold will almost certainly change. Google tends to lower barriers to new features as they gather data and build confidence in the system. The brand-building behaviors you establish now — structured entity signals, consistent brand presence, active Discover performance — will position you well whenever eligibility expands. This is one of those cases where building toward a future state is the right move even if you can't fully access the feature today.

How to Build Brand Signals That Feed Google's Trust Systems

Whether or not you can claim a Search Profile, the underlying game is the same: build brand signals that Google's systems can detect, verify, and trust. This sounds abstract, so let me get specific about what actually moves the needle in 2026.

"In a world where 72.4% of Google searches don't send anyone to a website, being found isn't enough anymore. Being recognized is what drives compounding traffic."

The single most impactful thing you can do is get your brand mentioned — not just linked — in high-authority publications. I know this sounds like standard PR advice dressed up in SEO clothing, but Google's language models and AI systems are trained to recognize brand-publication associations as authority signals. When Search Engine Land, a major industry newsletter, or a recognized vertical publication mentions your brand name in relevant context, that's a co-citation signal. It updates how AI systems understand your brand's credibility and relevance in a given topic space. This matters even when there's no hyperlink attached.

Second — and this is underrated — consistency of your brand identity across all platforms matters far more than it used to. Your brand name should be spelled identically on your website, your Google Business Profile, your LinkedIn company page, your Twitter/X bio, your YouTube channel name, and any industry directory listings you're in. Variations and inconsistencies — "Acme Co" vs "Acme Company" vs "ACME Corporation" — genuinely hurt entity clarity. Google's systems struggle to confidently merge these into a single entity, which dilutes every brand signal you're building.

Structured Data: The Entity Signals Google Can Read Directly

Schema markup has always mattered for rich results, but in 2026 it's taken on a second critical role: entity declaration. Using Organization schema on your homepage with sameAs links pointing to your social profiles, Wikidata entry, and company listings is telling Google directly: "These are all the same entity. This is who we are." Person schema on author pages does the same thing for the individuals writing on your site — establishing them as recognized experts with consistent identities across the web.

Nobody talks about this enough, but the data is pretty clear. Sites that have clean Organization and Person schema markup, connected to matching social profiles and Wikidata entries, get materially more Knowledge Panel appearances, more AI Overview citations, and more Discover traffic than comparable sites without it. The schema alone doesn't make or break your SEO, but it removes a major source of entity confusion that many sites are unknowingly suffering from.

  • Add Organization schema to your homepage with logo, founding date, description, and sameAs links to all major profiles
  • Add Person schema to every author page with matching name, bio, job title, and social profile links
  • Create or claim your Wikidata entity with basic brand information and a link to your site
  • Ensure your brand name is spelled identically across Google Business Profile, social bios, and your About page
  • Secure at least 3–5 editorial brand mentions in recognized publications in your niche within the next 90 days
  • Enable Google Search Console's AI Performance Reports to track how often your pages appear in AI Overview impressions
  • Monitor your competitors' brand signals to identify authority gaps you're not closing

One tactic that I've seen underused but consistently effective: internal linking between your brand's core identity pages and your highest-traffic content. Your About page, team pages, and key product or service pages should be contextually linked from your best-performing articles. This creates what I think of as brand authority pathways inside your site architecture — it signals to Google not just "this page covers this topic well" but "this page is connected to this recognized brand entity." In a world where AI systems are synthesizing content across many sources, entity clarity within your own site structure is genuinely important.

Your Brand Authority Action Plan for the Rest of 2026

Let me be direct with you here. The window to build brand authority before the next wave of AI search expansion is not as long as you might think. Google AI Mode is at 1 billion users and growing. The percentage of searches that result in open-web clicks is at 27.6% and has been trending down. Every month you treat SEO as purely a technical discipline — keywords, links, Core Web Vitals — without building your brand's recognition is a month your competitors who are playing the brand authority game pull further ahead in ways that compound and become harder to close.

The good news: most of the highest-impact brand authority actions are free or very low-cost. Here's the sequence I'd run starting this week:

1

Audit Your Entity Signals This Week

Run a full SEO audit on your site and look specifically at your Organization and Person schema, your brand name consistency across platforms, and whether your site has a Knowledge Panel. If you don't have a Knowledge Panel, that's your first priority target. The RankSorcery SEO Auditor will surface technical issues and schema gaps that are hurting your entity clarity right now — run it on your homepage first.

2

Create or Claim Your Wikidata Entity

Go to wikidata.org, search your brand name, and either claim an existing entry or create a new one. You need basic information: brand name, description (one sentence), founding date, headquarters, website, and your Wikipedia link if one exists. This takes 1–2 hours and is one of the highest-ROI entity-building tasks available to any site that hasn't done it yet.

3

Implement sameAs Schema on Your Homepage

Add or update your Organization schema markup with sameAs links pointing to your LinkedIn company page, Twitter/X profile, YouTube channel, Wikidata entry, and any other official profiles. This directly communicates to Google's knowledge graph that all of these are the same entity. It's a one-time technical task that takes 20–30 minutes and has lasting brand signal benefits.

4

Secure 3–5 Editorial Brand Mentions This Month

Reach out to industry publications, contribute expert quotes to journalists via services like Qwoted or HARO, or collaborate with recognized creators in your niche. You want co-citations — your brand name appearing in context alongside your core topics in publications that Google already trusts. Three real editorial placements this month beat thirty low-authority directory listings.

5

Track Branded Search in Google Search Console Weekly

Set up a filter in GSC for queries containing your brand name. This is your primary leading indicator of whether your brand authority work is having an effect. According to Nico Digital's research across 175+ clients, branded search volume lifts 60–90 days after AI engines start citing your brand more frequently. Flat or declining branded search is a signal that something in your entity signals needs attention.

If you do qualify for a Google Search Profile and haven't claimed it yet — stop reading this and go to creators.google/profile right now. I'm serious. Claiming your profile is free, it can trigger or significantly enhance your Knowledge Panel, and it creates a direct Discover traffic channel that most of your competitors haven't set up. There is no downside to claiming it if you're eligible, and several publishers have reported measurable Discover traffic increases within a few weeks of properly setting up their profiles.

For everyone else: the message here isn't that Google Search Profiles are the end goal. They're a signal of where search is heading. Google is increasingly organizing search around recognized entities — brands, publishers, and creators that it can verify, trust, and confidently surface to users who have shown interest in them. Whether you get there through a claimed Search Profile, a Wikidata entity, strong schema markup, or consistent editorial mentions, the destination is the same: becoming a brand that Google's systems recognize and trust enough to include in AI-generated answers and featured surfaces. That is the SEO game in the second half of 2026.

🚀
Bottom Line Google Search Profiles are a window into where all of search is heading — toward recognized entities over anonymous pages. Build your brand signals now: Wikidata entity, sameAs schema, editorial mentions, and consistent brand identity. Do this, and you'll be positioned well regardless of what Google's next AI update changes about how content surfaces.
